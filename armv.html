In 2008, a group of my friends and I started Architecture of Movement. All coming from different backgrounds, but united by our mutual passion for architecture, we saw problems in how our city was managed. Thousand year history and the status of Unesco heritage sight encouraged city officials to put their efforts into preserving the city, with little to no regards for the needs of the young people. We took on the challenge and sought new ways for urban development that would account for both, the future and the past.
A month long festival took place each September from 2008 to 2013 and focused on the interaction between citizens and the city space. Our goal was to create new opportunities and platforms for creative expression. We generated, accumulated and carried out ideas that helped develop the city as an open, modern and popular space. ARMV's program included cultural and urban workshops, multitude of dance and film performances, city walks, exhibits and many more. The results of the festival provided suggestions on how the city space could improve, how businesses and government organizations could collaborate, how cultural platforms could be expanded.

People and Rhythms

I curated a photo exhibit featuring works of Max Kutin, a photographer and film maker.

City Passport

This project curated by Natasha Khomutova enabled citizens to see the faces of the city and nurture bonds between neighbors. During the project, pedestrians were encouraged to share photographs of people they were proud of, who they thought represented the city generating a large collage that became the passport of Yaroslavl.

Workshops and Masterclasses

ARMV featured many workshops on urban planning and design, dance, social justice. The workshop on public street art by Henry Kendal, the director of NYC Department of Cultural Affairs, was one of the most talked about events of the year and sparked a movement of street artists.

Performances, screenings and walks

ARMV transformed many industrial, abandoned or simply underappreciated places into the art playgrounds. Our goal was to repurpose and breath in a new life into the spaces that otherwise would have been demolished or transformed into parking lots or highrises. This approach developed new communal space and encouraged support from the local businesses.

In 2010 I left the creative team to move to United States, but continued to participate as an independent artist.
